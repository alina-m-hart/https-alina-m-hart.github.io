Adoption Story

By Alina M. Hart

Alina M. Hart Hartford, CT Alina.m.hart.writer@gmail.com Word Count: 1,519

Adoption Story David and I had been waiting for this for a long time. We were financially secure, we had the nursery ready, and we had the love to give. But we didn't have a baby. When we met Stephanie, she was eight months pregnant and had already made the hardest decision a person can make. Everything was handled through an agency—legal, clean, and professional. We paid her medical bills and her legal fees, and David and I both took a full month off work so we could be there the moment the child was born. The plan was simple: a "closed" adoption. We lived in different states. We would take the baby home, and Stephanie would go back to her life. That was the agreement. When the call finally came that she was in labor, we drove like our lives depended on it. We ended up right there by her bedside in the hospital room. We coached her through the contractions, holding her hand and trying to be the support system she didn't have. But as the hours ticked by, Stephanie became unusually quiet. It wasn’t the kind of quiet that comes from exhaustion or the physical toll of labor. It was a heavy, suffocating silence. I watched her eyes, and I could tell she wasn't just thinking about the pain. She was counting down the minutes. She knew that in a few hours, she would be handing her child to us and walking away forever. I started thinking about all the things she was giving up. She would never see his first smile. She’d never see him take those wobbly first steps or hear him struggle through his first words. She was trading a lifetime of "firsts"—college, sports, weddings—for a future where he was safe and provided for. When the thoughts really started to hit her, Stephanie began to sob. It was a sound that went straight to my bones. A nurse came in, checking the monitors, and asked if she wanted an epidural. Stephanie just shook her head no.

The contractions were hitting her every sixty seconds now, but I could tell the pain in her chest was a thousand times worse. She was being devastated by her own choice, even though she knew why she was making it. I looked at David, then back at her. I felt a pang of guilt that was hard to swallow. I was about to gain everything she was about to lose. “Do you want to back out?” I asked her softly. I meant it. I didn't want to build my family on a foundation of pure destruction if she wasn't ready. She shook her head no again, but the tears didn't stop. I pulled my chair closer and sat right next to her bed. I tried to offer the only comfort I had. I told her I would take amazing care of him. I told her he would never go without anything, and that I would love him and protect him as if I had carried him myself. She seemed to find a little bit of peace in that, but the sadness stayed in the room like a physical weight. When it was finally time for the birth, Stephanie asked David and me to leave. We understood. We waited out in the hall, pacing the linoleum floors. David called our lawyer to let him know it was happening. About forty-five minutes later, the lawyer arrived, carrying a briefcase filled with the finality of the situation—the paperwork that would make that baby ours. Eight minutes after he was born, the nurse called us in. He was perfect. A healthy, beautiful baby boy. Stephanie didn't get to hold him. The nurse cleaned him up and placed him directly into my arms. I looked down at his tiny face, feeling a rush of love I can't even describe. But then, I looked over at the bed. The lawyer approached Stephanie with the final document.

The sound that came out of her then wasn't a scream. It was a gut-wrenching wail. It was the sound of a slow, agonizing surrender. Every breath she took sounded fractured, like her spirit was literally breaking open right in front of us. Her knees looked weak under the sheets, and her breath came in these shallow, shuddering gasps. In that moment, David and I finally understood the true magnitude of what she was doing for us. After the papers were signed, the lawyer ushered us out. We stood in the lobby, and I thanked the lawyer and said goodbye. But as we turned to leave, I felt a pull—a physical tug at my heart that I couldn't ignore. “I forgot my phone upstairs,” I told David. “I’ll go get it,” he offered, reaching for the bag. “No,” I said. “I know exactly where it is. I want to go.”

David asked if he could hold the baby while I ran up, but I gripped the car seat a little tighter. “No,” I whispered. “I want to hold him.”

I went back to the room. I hadn't forgotten my phone. I went back because I realized that as a mother, I couldn't let another mother end her story on a "gut-wrenching wail." She deserved a proper goodbye. When I walked back into the room with the baby, Stephanie reached out both arms. She let out a cry that broke my heart all over again. I handed him to her. She pressed her face against his tiny, warm cheeks and kissed his forehead. She whispered to him that she loved him more than anything in the world, more than she could ever explain. She asked him for his forgiveness and promised him that her love would always be with him. I stood there, watching them, and I couldn't help it. I imagined myself in her place. I imagined being the one who had to say goodbye. Without even thinking it through—without even talking to David—the words just came out. “Would you want this to be an open adoption?” I asked. Stephanie looked at me, completely stunned. “What do you mean?” “He should know you,” I said. “He will know you are his biological mother, and I will be the mother who raised him. We’ll have to change the papers. We’ll need rules and boundaries, obviously.” She was barely breathing, just staring at me. “You could see him on birthdays,” I continued, the ideas forming as I spoke. “Holidays. If you want to see him other times, you just let us know a month ahead of time.” “Yes,” she sobbed. “Yes. A thousand times yes.” Then a look of fear crossed her face. “Do you think David would agree?”

“I can convince him,” I said. “It’s in our son’s best interest. He needs to grow up knowing he wasn't abandoned. He needs to know he was loved by both of us.” I kissed the baby, said "see you soon" to Stephanie, and we hugged. It was the tightest hug I've ever experienced. When I got back to the car and told David, he was upset at first. It was a huge decision to make on the fly. But as I described the scene in the room—the depth of her grief and the way she held him—he started to soften. “I saw it too,” he admitted.

We talked about how important it was for our son to know his roots. We sat on the sofa that night with the newborn resting between us, and David took my hand. “Are you sure?” he asked. “I’m positive,” I said. “And I’m sorry I didn’t talk to you first.” “I get why you did it,” he said. “It was compassion. Just promise me from now on, we make the big calls together.” I promised.

We called the lawyer the next day and drafted the guidelines. We wanted to be careful, so we made sure we would always be present for the visits. Stephanie was beyond grateful. She told us she would have settled for one visit a year, but we gave her birthdays, Thanksgiving, Christmas, and video calls whenever she wanted. Watching her react to that news was the first time I saw her cry out of joy instead of pain. Over the years, Stephanie didn't just stay the "biological mother." She became a close friend. Our son, Sebastian, grew up knowing exactly who she was. He never had to wonder if he was unwanted. He knew he had two mothers who both adored him in their own ways. Sebastian grew up to be a successful family lawyer—maybe because of his own story. Stephanie eventually moved closer to us so she wouldn't have to travel so far for visits. Now, every Sunday, there’s an extra plate at our table. She’s not a guest; she’s family. And every time I see them talking together, I think back to that hospital room and the "phone" I supposedly forgot. It was the best lie I ever told.

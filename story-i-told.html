The Story I Told That Became True

By Alina M. Hart

When I was six years old, I lived in a small village in Belgium. My life was pretty simple. It was just me, my mother, and my three sisters. My father was living in the United States at the time, working hard so that eventually, we could all move there and be together. Our house ran on a quiet rhythm. We spent a lot of time waiting for letters and money to arrive from across the ocean.

I had a very vivid imagination back then. In my head, my mother wasn’t just a woman who stayed home and took care of us. I imagined her as someone much more worldly. In my mind, she was always boarding planes, visiting countries I had never seen, and coming back with all these beautiful, exotic objects. I told myself she owned a shop filled with treasures she had gathered from every corner of the globe. I really admired that version of her.

At school, I didn't keep these thoughts to myself. I told my classmates that this was exactly what my mother did for a living. I told them she was a traveler and a businesswoman. I think I just wanted her to have an exciting life, something people would be impressed by.

Everything was fine until one afternoon when my teacher, Madame Laurent, looked at me and said, “Take me to your mother. I would like to see what she sells.”

The words hit me before I even fully understood what they meant. I felt a cold chill. I lived too far from the school to walk home by myself, so the teacher decided she would walk home with me.

That walk was the longest walk of my life. With every step we took toward my house, my stomach tightened. The closer we got, the more nauseous I felt. It was this heavy, uneasy wave that just kept rising. My feet felt like lead, like the ground was trying to pull me down and stop me from reaching the front door. I really, really did not want to go home.

For a few seconds, I actually thought about running away. I wondered if I could just disappear into the neighborhood somewhere between the school and my house. But I wasn't a runner. I knew that even if I hid for an hour, I’d still have to face that teacher in class the next morning. So, I kept walking.

The path that usually felt so familiar now felt long and unforgiving. I could hear the sound of my own shoes on the pavement and the teacher’s footsteps right beside me—steady and patient. She had no idea about the storm that was building inside me. She was just expecting to see a shop full of treasures.

By the time we reached the door, I was desperate. I thought about lying again and saying my mother wasn't home, but I knew the teacher wouldn't believe me. She knocked.

My mother opened the door.

For a split second, I almost reached out for her. I wanted to whisper the truth, to tell her I had messed up and ask her to save me. But before I could get a word out, the teacher spoke up.

“Your daughter has been telling me that you travel and sell things,” she said. “I wanted to see what you have.”

My mother paused. I could see the surprise on her face, but she stayed completely calm. She didn't look at me with anger or confusion. She just took a breath and replied, “I haven’t traveled lately. I actually just sold all of my merchandise. I don’t have anything right now. When I restock, I will let you know.”

Relief washed over me so fast it was physical. It felt like my whole body finally released a weight it had been carrying since we left the school gate. My mother had covered for me without me even having to ask. She stood there smiling, and I stood there next to her, so incredibly grateful that I hadn't been embarrassed in front of my teacher.

After the teacher finally left, my mother sat me down and asked why I had told that story. The answer was simple, though it was hard to explain to an adult: I just wanted her to have an exciting life. I wanted her to be like someone I admired. My mother was kind about it, but she explained that lying wasn't right, especially when it caused someone to go out of their way for nothing. I apologized, and I meant it.

But even though I apologized, that image of the "traveler" never left me.

As I got older, the traveler in my mind stopped being my mother. It became me. In my imagination, I started seeing myself moving confidently through airports. I saw myself going to India, Europe, China, and Japan. I saw myself buying things, selling things, and learning about the world. In these visions, I saw a woman who lived independently. I wasn't married, I didn't have children, and I was surrounded by friendships I had gathered from all over the place.

Years later, I went to college and chose to study international business. I didn't consciously connect it to that story I told when I was six; it just felt like the natural thing to do. After I graduated, I started working in human resources, but it didn't take long for me to feel limited. I felt like I was stuck. Eventually, I moved into a role as a buyer. At first, it was for a small company, but later I moved to a much larger one that allowed me to travel internationally. I was finally meeting suppliers face to face, just like I had imagined.

I remember one evening very clearly. I had just finished a long business trip and I was sitting alone at a restaurant overlooking the ocean. The balcony was quiet. All I could hear was the sound of the waves hitting the shore below. A light breeze was blowing across the table. No one was sitting across from me—it was just an empty chair and the horizon stretching out forever.

As I looked out at the water, it hit me. I thought about how far I had traveled—not just across different countries, but across the years. A calm feeling settled over me. I realized I was living the exact life I had imagined when I was six years old. It wasn't something I had carefully planned out on a map or a calendar. It had simply unfolded, step by step, until I was there.

A few years ago, I was talking with my mother and I brought up that old memory.

“Do you remember when I was six and I told my teacher you traveled and sold things?” I asked her.

She smiled. She remembered it perfectly.

“I was just thinking about how similar my life turned out to that story,” I said.

My mother paused for a moment, thinking about it. Then she said, “You’re right. You became the world traveler.”

We sat there together in that realization. It was that same quiet understanding we had at the front door all those years ago—the woman who stood in my corner and the daughter who had grown into the life she imagined.

Later on, another realization came to me in a much more ordinary way. A colleague of mine announced she was pregnant. She had just gotten engaged and married, and she was so excited. I thought about my own life. I had been engaged more than once, but I had never taken that final step. I had always been the one to walk away.

I wondered about it, not because I felt regret, but just out of curiosity. I wondered how I had managed to step away each time. Would I have been happy if I had stayed? Would I have felt fulfilled in a different way? Sometimes I think I might have been a good mother. At fifty, I still don't have children.

That thought carries a small, passing sadness sometimes, but it’s never enough to make me want to rewrite my life. I have traveled the world. I have supported myself. I live independently and on my own terms. To me, self-reliance is everything. Maybe having both a family and that level of independence would have been nice, but the life I’ve lived is definitely not a mistake.

Looking back, I can see how that small story told by a six-year-old girl quietly shaped my entire direction. That "imagined truth" took root long before I was old enough to understand how powerful it was. Over time, it became my reality. It wasn't forced or over-planned; it was just lived.

Sometimes the stories we tell as children aren't lies at all. They are just early sketches of the lives we are quietly preparing to live.
